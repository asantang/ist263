The Classical Orders

These orders are recipes for architectural styles, they describe the layout ornamentation. These styles were applied to Greek temples. Doric - The simplest and least ornamented of the orders, robust dimensions which draw parallels to masculinity in terms of strength, stability, and fortitude. One side of Hellas - Strong Warriors. Ionic - Slightly more ornamentation, columns have slender proportions, and parallels are drawn to female elegance and complexity.From Ionia. Feminine slender. elegant proportions. One side of Hellas - Sophisticated Intellectuals Corinthian - The order created last, extremely ornamented. Death, Healing, Arts & Crafts. Young Woman Died, Tile put on a basket and eventually a plant grew over the surfaces, which gave inspiration to the capital design.The vines and palmette Leaves are associated with Dionysus, God of wine.

The Parthenon; The Caryatid

429 B.C.

Located in the Ancient city of Athens, home to The Parthenon, Propylaea, Erechtheion, Temple of Athena Nike. Money collected to support Greek efforts in the Persian war was used to build this monument to Athenian power. It's a citadel that overlooks the city. Views of the water to see approaching ships. A Temenos sacred space of Athena, Surrounding caves are full of snakes and trees- associated with Athena. Became a big piece of propaganda. Reflect the power of Athens, preserve its memory. Define the identity of Athens as the best part of Hellas To legitimize the hegemony of Athens over other cities. Power and cultural superiority cultural sophistication Parthenon - ​​built by Iktinos (architect), Kalikrates (contractor), Phidias (advisor) during the Classical Period. built to honor goddess Athena (she is the patron god of Athens). The Parthenon is an example of the Doric order. It is host to many optical refinements and visual manifestations of mathematics which perfectly proportion the building so it is as close to perfection as possible.

Gold Finger Ring Engraved with Image of Hermes

Late 4th century B.C.

Using the Intaglio technique of carving into the surface of a material, Hermes appears balancing as he fastens his wings. This piece is dated to the late 4th century. We can sense this late classical influence through the naturalistic rendering of Hermes’ figure as well as the breath holding action of his balancing pose.
